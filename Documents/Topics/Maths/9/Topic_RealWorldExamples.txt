### 1. The UPI Transaction You Do Every Day
Every time you pay ₹47.50 via UPI, the app breaks it into paise, processes it as a rational number (4750/100), and settles it across banks in milliseconds. But here's the surprise — when banks calculate interest on your balance overnight, the result is almost never a clean number. They store it as a real number internally and round only when displaying to you. The "₹0.01 interest" you see? That's a rounded irrational.

**Sub-topic link:** Rational numbers, operations on real numbers.

---

### 2. Why Your Aadhaar Card Exists
India stores biometric data for 1.4 billion people. The storage required is calculated using powers of 2 — laws of exponents. One fingerprint = ~1 KB = 2¹⁰ bytes. 1.4 billion people × 10 fingers = 1.4 × 10¹⁰ fingerprints. Engineers use exponent arithmetic daily just to plan how many servers UIDAI needs.

**Sub-topic link:** Laws of exponents, scientific notation.

---

### 3. The Rickshaw Driver Who Uses Irrational Numbers (Without Knowing It)
An auto driver takes the shortest cut across a rectangular colony — not along the roads but diagonally. That shortcut distance is always √(l² + b²). For a colony 300m × 400m, the diagonal is exactly 500m (a rare rational!), but for 300m × 500m it's √340000 ≈ 583.09…m — irrational. He saves time using a number he can't write as a fraction.

**Sub-topic link:** Irrational numbers, real numbers, operations.

---

### 4. Cricket DLS Method
When rain stops a match and Duckworth-Lewis-Stern kicks in, the revised target isn't guesswork — it's a mathematical formula that uses exponential functions. The "resources remaining" percentage involves real number calculations. That moment when the commentator says *"India need 47 off 5 overs under DLS"* — that number came from exponent-based math, not a gut feeling.

**Sub-topic link:** Laws of exponents, real numbers in applied context.

---

### 5. The Screen You're Reading This On
Your phone screen has millions of pixels. The diagonal size of a 16:9 screen (say 6.5 inches) is calculated as √(16² + 9²) × k — an irrational number. Phone manufacturers can't avoid irrational numbers when designing displays. Every "6.5-inch screen" measurement is technically irrational — rounded for marketing.

**Sub-topic link:** Irrational numbers, number line representation.

Chapter 2: Polynomials

---

### 1. The Jump in Every Video Game You've Played
When a character jumps in a game — BGMI, FIFA, any game — the arc of that jump is
programmed as a quadratic polynomial: h(t) = -5t² + v₀t. The moment the character lands,
h(t) = 0. The game engine is literally solving for the zeroes of a polynomial, 60 times
per second, to decide when to trigger the landing animation. The next time you see a
smooth jump arc, you're watching the zeroes of a degree-2 polynomial computed in real time.

Sub-topic link: Zeroes of a polynomial, polynomials in one variable.

---

### 2. Why Your WhatsApp Message Arrives Uncorrupted
Your message travels through towers, fibre cables, and satellite links — and bits get
flipped along the way. The reason it arrives intact is Reed-Solomon error correction,
used in every 4G and 5G network. It encodes your data as a polynomial, transmits it, and
checks the remainder when dividing by known factors at the receiving end. If the remainder
is zero, the message is clean. This is the Remainder Theorem — p(a) gives the remainder
when p(x) is divided by (x - a) — powering every mobile network in India.

Sub-topic link: Remainder theorem, factor theorem.

---

### 3. The Hidden Polynomial in Every Metro Arch
Look at the arches under any metro station in Delhi, Mumbai, or Bengaluru. That curve is
a parabola — described by ax² + bx + c = 0. The two feet of the arch, where it meets the
ground, are the zeroes of that polynomial. Structural engineers calculate those zeroes
to place foundations correctly, so the arch distributes load symmetrically. An error in
finding the zeroes means uneven stress — and eventually, cracks. The Factor Theorem gives
engineers a shortcut: if (x - r) is a factor, then r is a zero and a valid foundation point.

Sub-topic link: Zeroes of a polynomial, factor theorem, algebraic identities.

---

### 4. The Algebra Behind Every Amazon Sale Price
When e-commerce companies run pricing models — best price vs. stock vs. competitor —
they use polynomial regression. They fit a polynomial curve to historical sales data and
find the price point where profit is maximised (the peak of the curve) or where demand
drops to zero (the zero of the polynomial). The algebraic identity (a+b)² = a² + 2ab + b²
appears constantly when expanding the squared error terms in these models. Analysts in
Bengaluru and Hyderabad use these expansions daily.

Sub-topic link: Algebraic identities, zeroes of a polynomial.

---

### 5. How Animators at Indian VFX Studios Draw Smooth Curves
Every smooth curve in a Bollywood VFX shot — a swooping camera, a morphing shape — is a
Bézier curve, which is a polynomial of degree 2, 3, or higher. The control points that
animators drag in software like Maya or After Effects are literally the coefficients of
the polynomial. When an animator "smooths" a motion path, they're adjusting the polynomial
until it has the right shape. Splitting a curve at a point? That's the Remainder Theorem
applied to polynomial division. The craft of Indian animation runs on polynomial algebra.

Sub-topic link: Polynomials in one variable, remainder theorem, factor theorem.
